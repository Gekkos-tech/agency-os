## Persuasion frameworks

### AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action

The classic linear funnel inside a single ad.

- **Attention:** stop the scroll or the page-skim. Carried by the hook and the first visual.
- **Interest:** give a reason to keep watching/reading — a relevant fact, a relatable situation, a promise of payoff.
- **Desire:** shift from "interesting" to "I want that" — show the outcome, the transformation, the life with the product.
- **Action:** one clear, low-friction next step.

**When to use:** low-awareness audiences who don't yet know the product or category; launch campaigns; longer formats (30–60s video, advertorials) where you have room for all four beats. **Avoid when:** the audience already knows the product well — AIDA re-explaining feels patronizing; retargeting audiences need Desire/Action only.

**Failure mode to avoid:** spending 80% of the runtime on Attention gimmicks and rushing Desire. Desire is where conversion intent is built.

### PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solve

- **Problem:** name the pain precisely, in the audience's own words. Recognition is the hook ("that's me").
- **Agitate:** make the cost of the problem vivid — time lost, money burned, embarrassment, compounding consequences. Not fearmongering; honest consequence-mapping.
- **Solve:** introduce the product as the specific relief for the agitated pain. The solution must map 1:1 to the agitation — if you agitated wasted time, solve with time saved, not with "premium quality".

**When to use:** audiences who feel a pain but tolerate it (problem-aware, not solution-aware); services that remove pain rather than add pleasure (insurance, tools, B2B software, health). **Avoid when:** the product is pure pleasure/aspiration (perfume, travel, luxury) — agitating a "problem" feels manufactured; use identity/aspiration angles instead.

**Ethical guardrail:** agitation must describe real, plausible consequences. Never invent threats or exploit health/financial anxiety beyond what the product genuinely addresses.

### JTBD — Jobs-to-be-Done angles

People don't buy products; they "hire" them to do a job in a specific situation. The angle is the situation, not the product.

Build the angle from the job statement: **"When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."** Example: "When I'm cooking dinner with one hand holding a toddler, I want meals that need zero attention, so I can be present instead of stressed." The ad then dramatizes the situation and shows the product being hired.

Three job layers to mine:
- **Functional job:** the practical task (get from A to B, file taxes).
- **Emotional job:** how they want to feel while doing it (confident, unburdened).
- **Social job:** how they want to be seen (competent parent, savvy operator).

**When to use:** crowded categories where feature comparison is a losing game; products with non-obvious use cases; repositioning work. JTBD routes often uncover trigger moments (the "when" clause) that make targeting and creative feel eerily well-timed. **Avoid when:** the brief is a pure brand-awareness play with no concrete usage situation.

### 4U Headlines — Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific

A scoring rubric for headline-led routes. A strong headline scores on at least 3 of 4:

- **Useful:** promises a benefit the reader actually wants.
- **Urgent:** gives a reason to act now (time, scarcity, seasonality, cost of delay).
- **Unique:** says something competitors can't or don't say.
- **Ultra-specific:** numbers, names, timeframes. "Cut invoicing time by 71%" beats "invoice faster".

**When to use:** search ads, display, print, email subject lines, advertorial headlines — anywhere a headline does the whole selling job before a click. **Avoid when:** the channel is video-first and the hook is visual/spoken; then use the hook formulas below instead.

**Practice:** draft 5+ headline candidates per route, score each 0–4 on the U's, keep the top scorer. Urgency is the most commonly faked U — only claim it when real.

## Hook formula bank

The hook is the first 2 seconds of a video or the first line of copy. Its only job: earn the next 3 seconds. One formula per route; combine with a framework for the body.

### 1. Pattern interrupt
Break the expected visual or verbal pattern of the feed/category. Unexpected first frame, mid-action start, breaking the fourth wall, wrong-genre aesthetics (B2B ad shot like a nature documentary).
- *"POV: your accountant just saw your subscription list."* (starts mid-drama, no product in sight)
- Visual: a pristine product ad that begins with the product being thrown in the trash.
**Best for:** feed environments (Reels, TikTok, Shorts) with high scroll velocity. **Risk:** interrupt must connect to the message within seconds, or it's clickbait and retention collapses.

### 2. Open loop
Start a story or claim and withhold the resolution. The brain hates unclosed loops.
- *"We almost shut the company down over this one email."*
- *"There's a reason no mattress brand will show you this test."*
**Best for:** longer video, UGC-style ads, advertorials. **Rule:** the loop must actually close, and the payoff must be worth the wait.

### 3. Contrarian claim
Attack a belief the audience holds, or a rule the category preaches.
- *"Stretching before running is making you slower."*
- *"Stop A/B testing your buttons. It's not the buttons."*
**Best for:** expert/educated audiences, thought-leadership-adjacent brands, categories full of received wisdom. **Rule:** you must be able to back the claim in the body; contrarianism without evidence torches trust.

### 4. Social proof lead
Open with the crowd, a number, or a named authority — before any claim of your own.
- *"12,000 dentists switched last year. Here's what they know."*
- *"Rated 4.9 by people who hate leaving reviews."*
**Best for:** skeptical audiences, high-consideration purchases, retargeting. **Rule:** proof must be real and verifiable; invented numbers are a compliance and trust failure.

### 5. Before/after
Show the transformation instantly — the gap IS the hook.
- Visual: split screen, desk chaos → desk calm, timestamped.
- *"Monday: 47 open tabs. Tuesday: this."*
**Best for:** visual categories (fitness, cleaning, design tools, renovation), any product with a visible outcome. **Rule:** the "after" must be honest and typical, not a cherry-picked outlier (regulatory risk in health/finance categories).

## Combining framework × hook

| | Pattern interrupt | Open loop | Contrarian | Social proof | Before/after |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **AIDA** | Strong — interrupt = Attention | Good | Good | Moderate | Strong |
| **PAS** | Good | Strong — loop = agitation | Strong | Moderate | Strong — after = Solve |
| **JTBD** | Good — situation as interrupt | Strong | Moderate | Good | Good |
| **4U** | Weak — headline does the work | Moderate | Strong | Strong | Good |

## Emotional register palette (for the route diversity rule)

Rational proof · Identity/aspiration · Humor/pattern-break · Fear-of-loss/urgency · Warmth/care · Nostalgia · Rebellion/anti-category. Pick three distinct registers per brief; note that register is carried more by the visual idea and hook than by the framework.
