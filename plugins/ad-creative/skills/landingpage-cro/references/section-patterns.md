# Section Layout Patterns

Reference for the landingpage-cro skill. Proven structural patterns per section type — pick per audience and offer, don't default to the first. © GEKKOS Tech, MIT License.

## 1. Hero promise

**Pattern A — Classic split:** H1 + subline + CTA left, product visual right (stacked on mobile, visual second). Safe default for SaaS and services; the visual must show the product or outcome, not a stock metaphor.

**Pattern B — Centered statement:** oversized centered H1, one-line subline, single CTA, product visual full-width below. For strong, short value propositions and brand-confident offers; fails when the H1 needs explanation.

**Pattern C — Form-in-hero:** promise left, short lead form right (2–3 fields). For lead-gen with warm traffic (quotes, bookings, local services) — captures ready visitors instantly; too aggressive for cold mid-funnel traffic.

## 2. Credibility strip

**Pattern A — Logo bar:** 4–6 grayscale client/press logos, one quiet label ("Trusted by teams at" / "Bekannt aus"). B2B default; logos must be recognizable to THIS audience or they're wallpaper.

**Pattern B — Number strip:** 3 key figures with labels ("12,000+ customers · 4.9/5 on Google · since 2011"). Works when the brand lacks big-name logos; numbers must be real and sourced.

**Pattern C — Single review headline:** one strong short quote + star rating + platform logo, full-width. For consumer offers where one voice beats six logos.

## 3. Problem / solution

**Pattern A — Pain checklist → pivot:** 3–4 pain points as a checkmarked/x-marked list in the visitor's language, then a one-paragraph pivot introducing the mechanism. Fast to scan; best for problem-aware traffic.

**Pattern B — Before/after columns:** two columns, "Heute / Today" vs. "Mit [Product]", 3–4 mirrored rows. Makes the delta visual; best when the transformation is concrete and comparable.

**Pattern C — Narrative block:** short story format (3–5 sentences: situation → frustration → discovery). For high-empathy categories (health, coaching, family products); needs strong copy, hand specifics to ad-copy.

## 4. Offer detail

**Pattern A — Alternating feature rows:** 3–4 rows of visual + benefit-headline + 2 lines of text, alternating sides. SaaS/product default; every row headline states a benefit, not a feature name.

**Pattern B — Package card(s):** offer as 1–3 pricing/scope cards with included-items list and per-card CTA. For productized services and clear pricing; highlight one recommended card, max 3 options.

**Pattern C — Process timeline:** numbered 3–5 step "how it works" strip. For services and high-ticket offers where the buyer needs to see the path more than the feature list; each step names duration and what the customer does vs. what you do.

## 5. Social proof

**Pattern A — Testimonial trio:** 3 cards, each with photo, full name, role/company, and a result-specific quote ("cut invoicing from 3h to 20min"). Default. Specificity is the entire value — reject vague praise quotes.

**Pattern B — Case spotlight:** one deep customer story: photo/logo, 2–3 sentence narrative, 1–2 hard numbers pulled out large. For high-ticket B2B where one relevant peer beats ten strangers; choose the case matching the target segment.

**Pattern C — Review wall:** 6–9 short reviews in a masonry/grid with platform badges and dates. For consumer/e-commerce; recency and volume are the signal, keep individual reviews short.

## 6. Objection handling / FAQ

**Pattern A — Accordion FAQ:** 5–7 questions, collapsed, phrased as the visitor asks them ("What if it doesn't work with my system?" not "Compatibility"). Default; order by objection frequency, put price/contract questions in, not just softballs.

**Pattern B — Objection–answer pairs:** 3 hard objections stated boldly as headers with direct 2–3 line answers, no collapse. More confident than FAQ; best when there are exactly a few known killers (price, switching effort, trust).

**Pattern C — Comparison table:** product vs. status quo or vs. alternatives, 4–6 honest rows. For comparison-shopping audiences; concede a row you genuinely lose — total sweep tables read as propaganda.

## 7. Risk reversal

**Pattern A — Guarantee banner:** full-width strip with badge/icon + guarantee statement + one qualifying line ("30 Tage Geld-zurück. Ohne Formulare, ohne Diskussion."). Place directly before the final CTA.

**Pattern B — Trio of safety facts:** three icon+line items ("No credit card required · Cancel anytime · GDPR-compliant, hosted in EU"). For SaaS trials; each item must be literally true and specific.

**Pattern C — Founder note:** short personal note with photo and signature standing behind the promise. For small brands and services where the founder IS the trust anchor; skip for corporate brands.

## 8. Final CTA

**Pattern A — Recap CTA block:** benefit recap line (one sentence, mirrors the H1 from a new angle) + primary CTA + risk-reversal echo underneath. Default.

**Pattern B — Two-path close:** primary CTA plus explicit lower-commitment alternative side by side ("Start free trial" / "Or watch the 3-min demo first"). For high-consideration offers; the secondary path must feed a follow-up sequence, not a dead end.

**Pattern C — Form close:** the actual lead form embedded as the final section (short, labeled with what happens next: "We reply within 24h"). For lead-gen pages; repeat DACH data-reassurance line at the form.

## Combining patterns

- Don't stack three card-grid patterns in a row — alternate full-width, split, and grid rhythms so sections stay visually distinguishable (final visual treatment → design skills).
- Mobile order may differ from desktop order within a section (visual after text in heroes), never across sections — the narrative sequence is fixed.
- Every pattern above must survive the 390px test: if a pattern needs 3 columns to make sense (comparison tables), define its mobile collapse behavior explicitly (e.g. sticky first column, horizontal scroll).
